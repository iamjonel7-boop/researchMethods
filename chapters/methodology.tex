\chapter{Methodology}\label{methodology}

The research methodology comprises three-stage pipeline. It will
systematically process interoperability protocols from the technical
specifications towards the formal mathematical verification. Every stage
in this methodology is treated as sequential and dependent; the success
of every following stage directly follows from the output of the
previous stage. This ensures the logical connection of the whole
process.

\section{Formal Specification of Interoperability
Protocols}\label{formal-specification-of-interoperability-protocols}

The goal of this stage is to create a strict representation of
representative protocols using algebraic language. This formalisation is
important to remove details of their implementation that have no
connection to the semantic behaviour. This allows comparison between
asset-based and data-based mechanism.

\subsection{Procedure}\label{procedure}

\textbf{Select Representative Protocols:} Protocols that have wide
adoptation and formal documentation will be used. For asset-based,
Hashed Time-Lock Contracts (HTLC) will be used because it serves as the
de-facto guideline for atomic swap. For data-based, Oracle-based Message
Relay wil be used because of its fundamental paper in delivering
external data towards blockchains.

\textbf{Identify Actions:} Identify the elementary operations that makes
up each protocol: \textbf{Asset-Based \((A_{asset})\):} \emph{lock}
(locking of asset), \emph{unlock} (moving asset out using secret),
\emph{refund} (returning of token after a timeout). \textbf{Data-based
\((A_{data})\):} \emph{request} (asking of data), \emph{verify}
(confirmation of data integrity), \emph{relay} (delivery of proven
message to destination chain)

\textbf{Define Syntax:} Translate the actions into formal constants
using the language of process algebra (Algebra of Communicating
Process). Every action is treated as an event without duration
(instantaneous event).

\textbf{Encode Process:} Form the logical protocol using the following
operators: \textbf{Sequential (\(\cdot\)):} To signify strict sequential
process. \textbf{Alternative (\(+\)):} To model branching of decision.
\textbf{Parallel (\(||\)):} To model interaction between two different
blockchain ledger.

\textbf{Abstraction:} Use the \textbf{abstraction operator (\(\tau_I\))}
to hide internal operations (like cryptographic hashing or local state
updates) that are not seen by the other chain. The oberservable actions
that is critical to interoperability should be the only thing to be
left.

\section{Semantic Extraction Using Labelled Transition
System}\label{semantic-extraction-using-labelled-transition-system}

The goal of this stage is to give the process terms made from the
preceding stage formal interpretation using Labelled Transition System
(LTS). Through this, every algebraic term will have its corresponding
dynamic model that shows all possible observable behaviour of the
system.

\subsection{Procedure}\label{procedure-1}

\textbf{Lay out Structural Operational Semantics (SOS):} SOS will be
used in order to see the meaning of every operator in the process
algebra using inference rules. Every rule is composed of premise and one
conclusion.

\textbf{Construct the LTS Model:} For every specific protocol, LTS will
be formed as a triple \((S, A, \rightarrow)\): \textbf{States \((S)\):}
set of states comprising the original process term and all terms that
can be achieved from it (reachable states). \textbf{Labels \((A)\):}
labels of transition that refers to atomic actions that the system
executes. \textbf{Transition \((\rightarrow)\):} relation of transition
\((s \xrightarrow{a} s')\) that shows change from one state to the
another after the action \((a)\).

\textbf{Extract Traces at Reachable States:} \textbf{Reflexive States:}
reflexive transitive closure \((\rightarrow*)\) to identify all possible
states that can be reached by the protocol from the starting state.
\textbf{Traces:} get the finite sequences of actions
\(a_1, a_2, ..., a_n\) that the system executed in order to illustrate
the totally of its oberservable behaviour.

\section{Description of Observable
Behaviour}\label{description-of-observable-behaviour}

The goal of this stage is to formally identify the oberservable
behaviour of the interoperability protocols using the extraction of
traces and execution paths from the LTS mdoels. This survey will serve
as the basis of comparison of asset-based and data-based mechanims at
the noticeable level of their connecition.

\subsection{Procedure:}\label{procedure-2}

\textbf{Give meaning to Observable:} The behaviour of a system is tread
as oberservable if this comprises actions that can be seen or experience
by an outside observer or adjacent system by means of communication.
Under this theory of processes, all atomic actions that are not treated
as silent action (\(\tau\)) are all observable behaviour. Internal
calculatiosn or local changes in the storage that cannot be seen at
cross-chain communication will not be included in this survery.

\textbf{Extract Traces and Execution Paths:} \textbf{Traces:} Traces
\((Tr(q))\) will be extracted by listing all possible series of
oberservable actions that can be execute by the protocol from its origin
state (\(q_0\)). A trace is a string \(\sigma \in A^{}*\) where there
persist a sequential transitions towardsa new state. \textbf{Execution
Path:} In order to survey the structure of the protocol deeper,
execution paths or the total flow of the transition
\((s \xrightarrow{a} s')\) that shows not only the series of actions but
also points where there is nondeterministic choice or branch of
decision.

\textbf{Justify as Basis of Interoperability:} The oberservable
behaviour is the fundamental basis of interoperability because the
communication between two blockchain is being treated as a black box
interaction. In a chain\textquotesingle s perspective, the internal
implementation of the other chain has no corelation; what only matters
is the actions being sent and being accepted by it in order to make sure
the semantic consistency. In other words, the correctness of
interoperability depends on whether the observable traces of the two
system matches under the one specified protocol.

\section{Evaluation Criteria}\label{evaluation-criteria}

The goal of this stage is to set the formal guideline that will be used
in comparing asset-bsed and data-based protocols. By means of
identigying the level of similarity and difference of their behaviour,
results of the analysation under one concept of semantic
interoperability can be given interpretations.

\subsection{Criteria}\label{criteria}

\textbf{Similarity, Difference, and Equivalence:} \textbf{Equivalence:}
Two protocol is equivalent if their process graph shows equal pattern of
interaction to their environment. Formally, this is identified as an
equivalence class \((|x| = {y : x \sim y})\) wherein the system
belonging to this has one oberservable behaviour. \textbf{Similarity:}
This refers to having some parts of the trace matching even if the
structure of the process is not fully similar. \textbf{Difference:} This
surfaces when a protocol has branching structure or deadlock behaviour
that cannot be found in another even if they have matching strings of
action.

\textbf{Evaluation Criterias:} \textbf{Trace Equivalence \((=_{tr})\):}
This criteria will be used to survey if the protocols can execute
exactly the same series or strings of atomic action. Nonetheless, trace
equivalence is considered as coarse because this does not capture the
difference from deadlock or the timing of choices. \textbf{Bisimulation
Equivalence \((\sim)\):} This the stricter criteria (finest). Under this
criteria, two protocol need to have the same branching structure;
meaning, in every step of a protocol, the other should respond to the
same action and reach a state that is again equal.

\textbf{Interpretation of the Results:} The presence of bisimulation
between asset-based and data-based protocol means that the two has
semantic interoperability. That is, the meaning and context of their
operation still remains consistent in every transition.

If the protocols are trace equivalent only but not bisimilar, the
interpretation will have different level of liveness and safety; for
example, one fo them can lead to deadlock at situations where the other
one have not experienced yet.

The adherence to this formal criterias ensures that interoperability
does not only depend on technical connection but also to mathematical
correctness and semantic integrity of the system.
