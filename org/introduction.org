* Introduction
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: introduction
:END:
The blockchain ecosystem today has now evolved into its multi-chain era.
It is now characterized by a vast array of independent networks that
follows diverse technological and architectural approaches
[cite:@belenkov2025review]. The reason for this diversity is to
accomodate different needs or priorities for their different goals or
purposes. Designers make trade-offs between security, speed, and
decentralization. For example, while Ethereum serves as a mature,
primary ledger focused on liquidity and security, Solana on the other
hand utilizes a different consensus model to achieve high-throughput,
real-time verification. Another example is with the Specialized
platforms like Hyperledger Fabric.They further differentiate themselves
by offering "permissioned" environments with known identities, tailored
for enterprise sectors such as healthcare and supply chain management
where anonymity is impractical [cite:@alt2025distributed].

The disadvantage of this growing numbers of blockchain, however, is
starting to emerge. Their isolated nature has resulted in significant
fragmentation across the digital landscape [cite:@belenkov2025review].
This fragmentation creates liquidity isolation and restrictive user and
asset silos. This prevents us from seamlessly transferring value and
data between disparate blockchains [cite:@belenkov2025review]. As
decentralized applications become more complex and data volumes grow,
the inability of these "walled gardens" to communicate has become a
critical bottleneck
[cite:@wilson2026exploringblockchaininteroperabilityframeworks].
Addressing these silos is now viewed as an existential requirement for
the crypto economy. The success of the multi-chain future depends on the
reliable connectivity between these fragmented hubs. This fragmentation
has motivated extensive research and development in blockchain
interoperability, which is discussed next.

*** Interoperability
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: interoperability
:END:
Blockchain interoperability in is the ability of distinct and
independent blockchains to seamlessly exchange digital assets, data, and
arbitrary messages
[cite:@wilson2026exploringblockchaininteroperabilityframeworks]
[cite:@belenkov2025review] [cite:@singh2025comprehensive]. This is
essential if we want the blockchain ecosystems to be a cohesive network.
This will prevent the fragmentation of liquidity and utility across the
decentralized landscape
[cite:@wilson2026exploringblockchaininteroperabilityframeworks]. Through
this, complex applications can now function across multiple blockchains
[cite:@belchior2021survey]. Early visions for this connected future were
established by pivotal whitepapers, including Vitalik Buterin's (2016)
conceptualizations for cross-chain connectivity
[cite:@belenkov2025review] [cite:@Chaliasos2025-qs]. The Polkadot
network, envisioned by Gavin Wood (2016), introduced a heterogeneous
multi-chain framework where specialized "parachains" achieve
interoperability via a central relay chain [cite:@kim2018first]
[cite:@alt2025distributed]. Similarly, the Cosmos network, outlined by
Kwon and Buchman (2016), proposed an "internet of blockchains"
architecture using a hub-and-zone topology to facilitate trust-minimized
communication through the Inter-Blockchain Communication (IBC) protocol
[cite:@kim2018first] . These early frameworks established the
fundamental strategies ranging from lock-and-mint mechanisms to
light-client verification---that contemporary bridge protocols utilize
today [cite:@singh2025comprehensive].

*** Protocols
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: protocols
:END:
Existing interoperability solutions are typically classified based on
their technical architecture, trust model, and functional capability
[cite:@singh2025comprehensive]. Architectural classifications
distinguish between mechanisms such as lock-and-mint, burn-and-mint, and
liquidity networks. On the otherhand, trust-based models separate
"trusted" bridges from trustless or trust-minimized systems. The
difference is that the former rely on external validators or
multi-signaure sets meanwhile the latter utilize light clients or
zero-knowledge proofs [cite:@singh2025comprehensive]
[cite:@belenkov2025review].

From a functional standpoint, surveys categorize bridges by whether they
support "asset transfer," "data transfer," or both
[cite:@singh2025comprehensive]. However, it is critical to note that
these prevailing classifications are primarily feature-based. That is,
they focus on the operational mechanisms of message passing and
tokenization rather than the underlying semantics or the formal
correctness of the data being exchanged [cite:@kim2018first].

** Problem Statement
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: problem-statement
:END:
The most common significant risk in decentralised systems like
decentralised finance or DeFi ecosystem is in the security. This is
evident primarily in the cross-chain bridges. Security researchers
highlight that they are particularly vulnerable because they often store
vast amounts of collateral that back wrapped assets on secondary chains
[cite:@chainalysis2022bridgehacks] [cite:@belenkov2025review]. According
to Chainalysis, over $2 billion in cryptocurrency was stolen across 13
separate bridge hacks in 2022 alone. This accounted for approximately
69% of all funds stolen during that year
[cite:@chainalysis2022bridgehacks]. Another reports from CertiK and
Elliptic further emphasize that the intersection of on-chain smart
contract logic and off-chain communication components creates a
significantly larger attack surface than that of isolated decentralized
applications [cite:@ImmuneBytes_2023].

A recurring pattern can be seen from these systematic bridge failures.
These patterns centered on the violation of fundamental protocol
invariants [cite:@coinsbench]. A primary mechanism for these thefts is
the unauthorized minting of unbacked tokens. In this mechanism, the
attackers exploit the verification vulnerabilities of the system to
create assets out of thin air. These assets are created on the
destination chain without corresponding collateral on the source chain
[cite:@Nguyen_2022] [cite:@certik2022incident]. This creates imbalance.
Other incidents, such as the Nomad and Binance exploits, involved replay
attacks or proof manipulation. In these incidents, the bridge was
tricked into accepting fraudulent state updates
[cite:@belchior2021survey] [cite:@certik2022incident]. These real-world
failures represent a catastrophic breakdown in the conservation of
assets. Here, the mathematical equilibrium that should exist between
disparate ledgers was completely severed [cite:@coinsbench]
[cite:@Chaliasos2025-qs] [cite:@maric2025formal]. These failures reveal
that current interoperability approaches often lack clear semantic
guarantees about what must always hold true during cross-chain
operations.

Semantic compatibility is explicitly identified in the literature as a
primary open challenge for blockchain connectivity
[cite:@belchior2021survey]. This presents a significant barrier to the
maturation of the multi-chain ecosystem. Specifically, current
frameworks often facilitate the verification and transportation of data
between chains without transporting the semantics of its proper use.
This leaves a critical gap at the protocol/semantic interface.

There is currently no unified model that formally separates these two
categories based on the specific state-machine transitions and safety
properties that they must preserve. This research addresses this problem
by formally modelling cross-chain interoperability to demonstrate that
data and asset transfer belong to different semantic classes.
Specifically, this research aims to answer the following research
questions:

1. How can we model cross-chain interactions as unified state transition
   system across a multiple replicated state machines?

2. What invariants and transition properties formally characterises
   asset interoperability in cross-chain systems?

3. What invariants and correctness properties formally characterises
   data interoperability in cross-chain systems, and how do they differ
   from asset interoperability?

** Objectives
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: objectives
:END:
In order to address the lack of formal semantic distinction between
asset and data interoperability, this research proposes the following
objectives:

*** Main Objective
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: main-objective
:END:
To develop a formal semantic framework that distinguishes asset from
data interoperability in cross-chain systems using state-machine
modeling and invariant analysis to provide rigorous safety guarantees
for multi-chain interactions.

*** Specific Objectives
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: specific-objectives
:END:
- To formalize asset interoperability semantics by defining state
  variables, transition functions, and conservation invariants, which
  are necessary to ensure the mathematical equilibrium of tokens across
  disparate ledgers

- To model data interoperability semantics by specifying state variables
  for arbitrary message passing, rules for cross-chain delivery, and
  integrity properties, which ensure that data remains synchronized and
  correctly interpreted across heterogeneous environments.

- To conduct a comparative analysis of the invariant structures for
  asset and data transfer to identify fundamental semantic differences,
  specifically focusing on how safety, liveness, and fault tolerance are
  uniquely preserved in each category.

- To demonstrate the framework's utility by mapping existing
  interoperability protocols and their documented failure modes (such as
  the Wormhole and Nomad exploits) to the proposed semantic categories
  to verify their adherence to fundamental protocol invariants.

** Scope and Limitations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: scope-and-limitations
:END:
This research focuses on the underlying logical properties rather than
the implementation details or empirical performance of certain
blockchain protocols.

*** Scope
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: scope
:END:
This research will use state-machine modelling invariant analysis to
formalise how information and value move between independent ledgers
plus identify and verify critical protocol invariants like conservation
of assets and message integrity. This research will analyse established
patterns in asset and data interoperability in order to develop a
unified model to bridge the gap between protocol-level details and
semantic-level interoperability. It will not propose new
interoperability protocols nor provide functional bridge
implementations.

This research will not benchmark metrics such as transactions per second
(TPS), latency, or gas efficiency. It will not analyze the internal
mathematical robustness of cryptographic primitives such as secp256k1
and SHA2-256. It will not focus on providing machine-checked proofs in
languages like Coq or Isabelle/HOL; but rather on framework definition
and manual formalization. It will not consider incentive compatibility
as well such as validator reward structures, or the economic cost of
attacks. Lastly, legal and compliance frameworks, such as AML/KYC
requirements or jurisdictional laws, are outside the scope of this
technical analysis,.

*** Limitations
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: limitations
:END:
The proposal models in this research are abstract and minimal by design.
They prioritize the "core logic" of state transitions and may not
capture every low-level implementation detail of real-world protocols.
This research limits the analysis to a selected set of representative
protocols like Ethereum, Cosmos and Wormhole. It is not intended to be
an exhaustive survey of all existing bridging solutions. This research
does not implement testing on live testnets or mainnets; the
verification remains at the formal specification level. Lastly, the
findings are primarily theoretical and intended to inform design
patterns. The practical deployment may require further adaptation to
account for off-chain behaviors and network synchrony.

** Significance
:PROPERTIES:
:CUSTOM_ID: significance
:END:
This research fills a critical gap in existing literature by providing
the formal semantic distinction between asset and data interoperability.
It contributes a foundational abstraction for interoperability research.
Thus, it directly address the "understudied aspect" of semantic
compatibility identified in major surveys. By defining the
protocol/semantic interface, this work enables more precise protocol
classification and comparison than the prevailing feature-based
taxonomies. Furthermore, the clarity that this framework provides by
specifying the invariant requirements that protocols must uphold, future
formal verification will be easier and robust. Thus also helps provides
rigorous basis for automated reasoning in distribute systems.

From a practical standpoint, this framework also helps protocol
designers and architects. This research will help them understand the
distinct semantic requirements of different interoperability models. It
will clarify the technical necessity for conservation guarantees in
asset transfers while establishing that data transfers must prioritize
integrity and schema alignment. By formalizing these requirements, the
research aims to prevent design errors that have historically led to
multi-million dollar bridge vulnerabilities, such as the $320 million
Wormhole exploit and the $190 million Nomad hack. Additionally, it
provides essential conceptual clarity for security analysis and
auditing, moving the industry toward standardized security best
practices and safer cross-chain communication protocols.
