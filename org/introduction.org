Blockchain interoperability in is the ability of distinct and
independent blockchains to seamlessly exchange digital assets, data,
and arbitrary messages
[cite:@wilson2026exploringblockchaininteroperabilityframeworks]
[cite:@belenkov2025review] [cite:@singh2025comprehensive]. This is
essential if we want the blockchain ecosystems to be a cohesive
network. This will prevent the fragmentation of liquidity and utility
across the decentralized landscape
[cite:@wilson2026exploringblockchaininteroperabilityframeworks].
Through this, complex applications can now function across multiple
blockchains [cite:@belchior2021survey]. Early visions for this
connected future were established by pivotal whitepapers, including
Vitalik Buterin's (2016) conceptualizations for cross-chain
connectivity [cite:@belenkov2025review] [cite:@Chaliasos2025-qs]. The
Polkadot network, envisioned by Gavin Wood (2016), introduced a
heterogeneous multi-chain framework where specialized "parachains"
achieve interoperability via a central relay chain

[cite:@kim2018first] [cite:@alt2025distributed]. Similarly, the Cosmos
network, outlined by Kwon and Buchman (2016), proposed an "internet of
blockchains" architecture using a hub-and-zone topology to facilitate
trust-minimized communication through the Inter-Blockchain
Communication (IBC) protocol [cite:@kim2018first] . These early
frameworks established the fundamental strategies ranging from
lock-and-mint mechanisms to light-client verification---that
contemporary bridge protocols utilize today
[cite:@singh2025comprehensive].



From a functional standpoint, surveys categorize bridges by whether
they support "asset transfer," "data transfer," or both
[cite:@singh2025comprehensive]. However, it is critical to note that
these prevailing classifications are primarily feature-based. That is,
they focus on the operational mechanisms of message passing and
tokenization rather than the underlying semantics or the formal
correctness of the data being exchanged [cite:@kim2018first].

The most common significant risk in decentralised systems like
decentralised finance or DeFi ecosystem is in the security. This is
evident primarily in the cross-chain bridges. Security researchers
highlight that they are particularly vulnerable because they often
store vast amounts of collateral that back wrapped assets on secondary
chains [cite:@chainalysis2022bridgehacks] [cite:@belenkov2025review].
According to Chainalysis, over $2 billion in cryptocurrency was stolen
across 13 separate bridge hacks in 2022 alone. This accounted for
approximately 69% of all funds stolen during that year
[cite:@chainalysis2022bridgehacks]. Another reports from CertiK and
Elliptic further emphasize that the intersection of on-chain smart
contract logic and off-chain communication components creates a
significantly larger attack surface than that of isolated
decentralized applications [cite:@ImmuneBytes_2023].

** Problem Statement


A recurring pattern can be seen from these systematic bridge failures.
These patterns centered on the violation of fundamental protocol
invariants [cite:@coinsbench]. A primary mechanism for these thefts is
the unauthorized minting of unbacked tokens. In this mechanism, the
attackers exploit the verification vulnerabilities of the system to
create assets out of thin air. These assets are created on the
destination chain without corresponding collateral on the source chain
[cite:@Nguyen_2022] [cite:@certik2022incident]. This creates
imbalance. Other incidents, such as the Nomad and Binance exploits,
involved replay attacks or proof manipulation. In these incidents, the
bridge was tricked into accepting fraudulent state updates
[cite:@belchior2021survey] [cite:@certik2022incident]. These
real-world failures represent a catastrophic breakdown in the
conservation of assets. Here, the mathematical equilibrium that should
exist between disparate ledgers was completely severed
[cite:@coinsbench] [cite:@Chaliasos2025-qs] [cite:@maric2025formal].
These failures reveal that current interoperability approaches often
lack clear semantic guarantees about what must always hold true during
cross-chain operations.

Semantic compatibility is explicitly identified in the literature as a
primary open challenge for blockchain connectivity
[cite:@belchior2021survey]. This presents a significant barrier to the
maturation of the multi-chain ecosystem. Specifically, current
frameworks often facilitate the verification and transportation of
data between chains without transporting the semantics of its proper
use. This leaves a critical gap at the protocol/semantic interface.

** Objectives
In order to address the lack of formal semantic distinction between
asset and data interoperability, this research proposes the following
objectives:

*** Main Objective
To develop a formal semantic framework that distinguishes asset from
data interoperability in cross-chain systems using state-machine
modeling and invariant analysis to provide rigorous safety guarantees
for multi-chain interactions.
*** Specific Objectives
- To formalize asset interoperability semantics by defining state
  variables, transition functions, and conservation invariants, which
  are necessary to ensure the mathematical equilibrium of tokens
  across disparate ledgers

- To model data interoperability semantics by specifying state
  variables for arbitrary message passing, rules for cross-chain
  delivery, and integrity properties, which ensure that data remains
  synchronized and correctly interpreted across heterogeneous
  environments.

- To conduct a comparative analysis of the invariant structures for
  asset and data transfer to identify fundamental semantic
  differences, specifically focusing on how safety, liveness, and
  fault tolerance are uniquely preserved in each category.

- To demonstrate the framework's utility by mapping existing
  interoperability protocols and their documented failure modes (such
  as the Wormhole and Nomad exploits) to the proposed semantic
  categories to verify their adherence to fundamental protocol
  invariants.

** Scope and Limitations

This research focuses on the underlying logical properties rather than
the implementation details or empirical performance of certain
blockchain protocols.
*** Scope

This research will use state-machine modelling invariant analysis to
formalise how information and value move between independent ledgers
plus identify and verify critical protocol invariants like
conservation of assets and message integrity. This research will
analyse established patterns in asset and data interoperability in
order to develop a unified model to bridge the gap between
protocol-level details and semantic-level interoperability. It will
not propose new interoperability protocols nor provide functional
bridge implementations.

This research will not benchmark metrics such as transactions per
second (TPS), latency, or gas efficiency. It will not analyze the
internal mathematical robustness of cryptographic primitives such as
secp256k1 and SHA2-256. It will not focus on providing machine-checked
proofs in languages like Coq or Isabelle/HOL; but rather on framework
definition and manual formalization. It will not consider incentive
compatibility as well such as validator reward structures, or the
economic cost of attacks. Lastly, legal and compliance frameworks,
such as AML/KYC requirements or jurisdictional laws, are outside the
scope of this technical analysis,.

*** Limitations

The proposal models in this research are abstract and minimal by
design. They prioritize the "core logic" of state transitions and may
not capture every low-level implementation detail of real-world
protocols. This research limits the analysis to a selected set of
representative protocols like Ethereum, Cosmos and Wormhole. It is not
intended to be an exhaustive survey of all existing bridging
solutions. This research does not implement testing on live testnets
or mainnets; the verification remains at the formal specification
level. Lastly, the findings are primarily theoretical and intended to
inform design patterns. The practical deployment may require further
adaptation to account for off-chain behaviors and network synchrony.

** Significance

This research fills a critical gap in existing literature by providing
the formal semantic distinction between asset and data
interoperability. It contributes a foundational abstraction for
interoperability research. Thus, it directly address the "understudied
aspect" of semantic compatibility identified in major surveys. By
defining the protocol/semantic interface, this work enables more
precise protocol classification and comparison than the prevailing
feature-based taxonomies. Furthermore, the clarity that this framework
provides by specifying the invariant requirements that protocols must
uphold, future formal verification will be easier and robust. Thus
also helps provides rigorous basis for automated reasoning in
distribute systems.

From a practical standpoint, this framework also helps protocol
designers and architects. This research will help them understand the
distinct semantic requirements of different interoperability models.
It will clarify the technical necessity for conservation guarantees in
asset transfers while establishing that data transfers must prioritize
integrity and schema alignment. By formalizing these requirements, the
research aims to prevent design errors that have historically led to
multi-million dollar bridge vulnerabilities, such as the $320 million
Wormhole exploit and the $190 million Nomad hack. Additionally, it
provides essential conceptual clarity for security analysis and
auditing, moving the industry toward standardized security best
practices and safer cross-chain communication protocols.
